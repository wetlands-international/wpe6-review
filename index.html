Introduction

Information on the world’s waterbird populations, with estimates, trends and 1% values is available on the Waterbird Population Estimates portal. This information is used as the definitive source by Ramsar Convention, African Eurasian Waterbird Agreement, East Asian - Australasian Flyway Partnership, Western Hemisphere Shorebird Reserve Network and for many other conservation purposes.

Wetlands International is developing boundary maps for biogeographic populations of all waterbird species to be included in the new global Waterbird Population Portal (WPP), scheduled for launch in late 2020. For information on the WPP, see here.

Boundary maps are available for 1,833 of the 2,348 of waterbird populations recognised in the portal. The boundary maps include those finalised for the African Eurasian region in the Critical Site Network Tool and draft maps prepared by Wetlands International, Wildfowl & Wetlands Trust (UK), Manomet, Inc. and our other partners. For a description of biogeographic populations and their delineation adopted by the WPP, see here.

Consultation process

The main source document for waterbird populations and definitions used for this work is available online on the Waterbird Population Estimates portal. We invite your review of the maps and suggestions to refine these proposed boundaries. Specific suggestions to boundaries need to be supported on the basis of information on (a) known movements of birds (from colour marking or metal band/ring movements or telemetry studies); or (b) known distribution of birds outside the boundaries.

To provide your input, we have the following options:

  • Click on the boundary you wish to provide feedback for and then click the download link to obtain a .kml file of it. You can edit this file using either your own GIS software or the excellent online service geojson.io. Then email to us, email details provided below.
  • Save the map for a species on to a computer (using PrintScreen). Use any suitable software (Microsoft Paint) to annotate proposals for changes on the map. Email to us, email details provided below.
  • Save the map for a species on to a computer (using PrintScreen). Print the map and make annotations with a pen to indicate suggested changes. Scan the map and email to us, email details provided below.

Important Note

Kindly note that these are draft maps are solely developed and shared for the purpose of consultation. They should not be used in this form as definite boundaries for the concerned species and their populations.

It is anticipated that a final version of the maps will be made public with the launch of the WPE6, which is intended to take place by end 2018.

Many thanks for your input.
